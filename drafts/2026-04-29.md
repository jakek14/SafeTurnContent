8 states now require MCA lenders to show you the APR before you sign.

Sounds like protection.

It's not.

Knowing the rate tells you what the advance costs.

It doesn't tell you what happens to your cash flow when it's stacked on top of two others.

Here's what the disclosure doesn't show:

Each lender underwrites their advance in isolation. They look at your revenue, your bank statements, and they approve their deal.

They're not calculating the combined daily pull on your operating account.

That math is yours to figure out.

So a business owner sees a 1.3 factor rate, runs the numbers, and thinks — $1,700 a day. Manageable.

But there are already two advances pulling a combined $2,400 a day out of the same account.

The disclosure was there. Everything was legal. Fully transparent.

And now the business is clearing $4,100 a day just to service debt — before a single dollar reaches operations, payroll, or growth.

Transparency rules protect lenders from liability.

They don't protect your cash flow from math.

The only way to change that picture is to stop looking at each agreement individually and see the total obligation structure all at once.

That's what restructuring actually is.

If the individual terms look reasonable but the combined weight is unsustainable — that's not a disclosure problem.

It's a structure problem.
