Tariffs didn't kill this business.

The debt structure they built to survive them did.

Imagine a wholesale distributor doing $3.5M a year.

Solid margins. Strong buyer relationships. Nothing fancy — just a business that works.

Then imported inventory costs spike 25% overnight.

Buyer contracts lock pricing for 90 days. They can't pass the cost through immediately.

So they take an MCA to bridge the gap. $80K at a 1.3 factor. That's $104K total payback — about $790/day over 6 months.

Revenue holds. Prices adjust. Problem solved, right?

Not quite.

The next inventory cycle hits before the advance is paid off.

Cash is thin — $790 has been pulling from the account every business day.

So they take a second MCA to cover the reorder. $60K more. Another $590/day.

Now $1,380/day combined. Every business day.

That's over $30,000 a month leaving the account before payroll, rent, or anything else.

The business is generating real revenue. Margins are tight but real.

One slow quarter and there's nothing left to absorb the hit.

This is the cycle rising bankruptcies are revealing right now — not businesses that made bad decisions, but businesses whose structure couldn't absorb a temporary shock without compounding it.

Restructuring both advances — extending the terms, reducing the daily pull — doesn't erase the debt.

It just gives the business room to operate while paying it down.
