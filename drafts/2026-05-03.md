Two or three MCA advances isn't just a cash flow problem.

It's a coordination problem.

Most business owners don't realize this until they're already in trouble.

Each advance is its own independent contract. Its own UCC filing. Its own collection timeline. Its own leverage.

These lenders aren't working together. Each one is trying to collect as fast as possible — on their own schedule, with no regard for the others.

That's manageable when business is running well.

When cash gets thin, it's a different situation entirely.

Each lender can act independently. One of the tools available to them: a UCC 9-406 notice — a legal mechanism that redirects your incoming receivables directly to them, bypassing your bank account entirely.

Revenue doesn't land in your account. It goes straight to the lender.

Meanwhile, the other advances keep pulling from whatever is left.

This is why "just negotiate with your lenders" isn't as simple as it sounds.

You need all of them at the table. Not most — all.

If even one holds out, the others' agreements don't protect you.

Business owners with stacked MCA debt aren't managing one problem.

They're managing several independent parties — each with their own leverage, each with no obligation to cooperate with the others.

That's a structure problem, not a revenue problem.
