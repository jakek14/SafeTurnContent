Small business reorganization filings jumped 91% in February 2026 vs. a year ago.

Not 10%. Not 25%. Ninety-one percent.

These aren't businesses that suddenly ran out of money.

Most of them were managing 12 to 18 months ago.

That's the part worth understanding.

The path to a formal filing almost never starts with a crisis. It starts with a gap.

A slow quarter. A late-paying client. A payroll week that doesn't quite line up with cash landing.

So the business finds capital to bridge it. Makes sense.

But each new obligation adds more pressure on the same operating account.

The next gap is tighter. Margins compress. Options narrow.

There's an invisible threshold in this cycle — and most operators don't realize they've crossed it until they're past it.

That threshold is where voluntary restructuring starts getting harder.

Before it: terms can be renegotiated, payments realigned, cash flow opened back up.

After it: fewer options, higher costs, harder decisions.

What separates businesses that make it through from those that file isn't revenue.

It's catching the structure problem before it becomes a formal one.

If your obligations feel heavier than your revenue can support — that's the signal.

Not the filing. Not the collection calls. The widening gap between what's coming in and what's going out.
