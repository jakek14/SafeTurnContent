Businesses most likely to file bankruptcy aren't the ones avoiding debt.

They're 3-4x more likely to be actively applying for more of it.

That's not a guess. That's what credit data shows about at-risk companies in the months before they file.

The instinct makes sense.

Cash is tight. A gap needs to be filled. So you find more capital to bridge it.

But here's the problem: most cash flow gaps aren't caused by a lack of money.

They're caused by a payment structure that drains cash faster than the business generates it.

Adding another advance on top of that structure doesn't fix the problem.

It accelerates it.

Every new obligation adds another daily or weekly withdrawal. More cash leaving the account before revenue lands. Tighter margins. Less flexibility.

And the next time cash gets tight — which it will — the debt load is higher, the options are fewer, and the cost to fix it is greater.

More debt is not the answer to a debt problem.

Restructuring what you already owe is.

That means renegotiating terms. Consolidating obligations. Aligning payments with what cash flow can actually support — not what a lender was willing to approve.

Businesses that survive a cash flow crisis aren't the ones that out-borrowed the problem.

They're the ones that stopped layering on obligations and started fixing the structure underneath.

If your instinct right now is "I just need more capital" — it's worth pausing.

Is the problem really a shortage of cash?

Or a structure that's consuming what you already have?
