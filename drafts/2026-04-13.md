Picture this.

A staffing agency doing $5M/year. Real clients. Consistent revenue.

Just got denied by their bank.

Not because the business is failing.

Because the MCA stack on their books looked like too much of a liability to lend against.

Here's how it happened:

Staffing has a cash flow gap baked in.

You pay workers every week.
Clients pay in 30 to 60 days.

That gap gets covered with a line of credit. Then an MCA when the credit line maxes out.

Daily payments: $1,400.

Q1 hits slow. Placements drop. Clients drag invoices to 60 days.

A second advance to cover payroll.

Now two MCAs. $2,600 pulling every business day.

That's $57,200 leaving the account each month — before rent, benefits, or anything else.

The owner goes to the bank.

Denied.

The reason: "Too much existing debt."

The business is fundamentally healthy. Real clients, real revenue, real demand.

But the obligation stack makes it look otherwise to every traditional lender reviewing the file.

This is the corner that catches operators off guard.

More revenue won't fix this. The structure is the problem.

Restructuring those two obligations into terms that align with the actual billing cycle doesn't change what's owed.

It changes when those payments leave the account.

And that difference is everything.
