The MCA was supposed to be a bridge.

Three months. Cover the cost spike. Get back to normal.

Picture a wholesale distributor doing $5M/year.

Strong margins. Steady clients. 12 years in business.

Then tariffs hit their primary supplier category. Costs jump — but client contracts are locked in for the quarter. They can't raise prices fast enough.

So they take an MCA. $130K. Approved in 72 hours. Daily payments: $900.

Month three, margins start recovering. But the daily drain has kept cash so tight they can't rebuild any buffer.

A second advance. Another $80K to stay ahead of operating expenses.

Now $1,500/day is leaving the account. Every business day. Before payroll, rent, or anything else.

That's $33,000/month in MCA payments — on a business that still has strong revenue.

The original cost problem lasted one quarter.

The debt structure created to solve it is still running two years later.

This is the pattern that shows up in businesses that look fine on paper but feel impossible to run.

Revenue isn't the issue.

The issue is a short-term structure that became a long-term obligation — and no one ever stopped to restructure it.

Extending terms and reducing the daily pull can cut the monthly drain by half without touching total principal.

Same debt. Completely different business.

If this sounds familiar, the structure is the right place to start.
