Most seasonal businesses don't fail in their slow season.

They fail because of what they borrowed to survive it.

Picture a landscaping company doing $2M a year.

Revenue hits hard in spring and summer. By November, the accounts are running thin.

They take an MCA to cover payroll through the slow months.

The advance was sized on average monthly revenue — not the seasonal reality of how the business actually earns.

Daily withdrawals continue through January, February, March.

When April comes and revenue picks back up, the advance still isn't paid off.

So they take another one to cover spring startup costs — equipment, materials, crew ramp-up.

Now they're entering their busiest season carrying two advances, both drawing against the same account that needs to fund operations.

They work their best quarter of the year.

The profit margin that was supposed to rebuild cash reserves went to debt service.

By October, same position as last year.

Except now the banks see more obligations. The MCA terms are tighter. And the window for restructuring is smaller.

This isn't a bad business. It's a seasonal business with the wrong debt structure.

The fix isn't a better summer.

It's restructuring the timing of what's owed to match how the business actually earns — so debt payments don't consume the quarters that were supposed to fund the rest of the year.

Debt aligned to your cash cycle changes the math entirely.
