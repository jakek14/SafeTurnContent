15 years in business. $8M in annual revenue. Rock-solid client relationships.

And the owner is 90 days from shutting down.

Here's how it happens.

Imagine a wholesale distributor. Imports specialty materials. Steady contracts with contractors and builders across three states.

When tariff costs jumped earlier this year, margins got compressed overnight.

Not catastrophically. But enough to hurt.

They bridge the gap with an MCA. $150K. Daily payments: $900.

Then a key client slows their orders while adjusting their own budget.

Revenue dips 20% for two months.

Not a disaster. But $900/day doesn't pause for slow months.

So they take a second MCA to cover the gap.

Now: $1,600/day leaving the account. Automatically. Every business day.

That's $35,200 a month in debt service — before payroll, rent, freight, or inventory.

The business isn't broken. It's suffocating.

MCAs are built for short-term gaps. When you stack them, they become a permanent drain on a business that was never designed to carry that kind of weight.

Restructuring both obligations — extending terms, aligning payments with the actual cash cycle — can cut that monthly burden by more than half.

Same total debt. The business gets room to breathe.

15 years of work doesn't have to end because of 6 months of structural pressure.
