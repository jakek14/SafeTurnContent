Picture a staffing agency doing $3M a year.

Full client roster. 40 employees placed with clients every week.

On paper, things are working.

But every Friday is a crisis.

Here's what's happening:

Employees get paid weekly. Clients pay net 45.

That's a 45-day gap between money going out and money coming in. Every single week.

So the owner takes an MCA to bridge it. $120K. Daily payments: $900. Approved in 48 hours.

The gap closes. Until a major client runs 30 days late on a big invoice.

Now a second MCA. $80K more.

Daily payments jump to $1,700.

Every business day. Automatically pulled.

That's $37,400 leaving the account every single month — before payroll, before taxes, before rent, before anything.

A business doing $3M a year, and the owner is sweating every Friday wondering if there's enough in the account.

This is a structure problem. Not a revenue problem.

The timing mismatch between billing and collection is real. But fixing it with a daily-repayment product doesn't close the gap — it just adds another daily obligation on top of it.

The fix isn't more clients or higher revenue.

It's restructuring both obligations into a schedule aligned with how the business actually gets paid — so payments stop fighting the cash cycle instead of working with it.

Same debt. Same business. Completely different experience.
