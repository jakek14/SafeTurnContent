An MCA is not a loan.

That distinction explains almost everything about why it's so hard to get out from under one.

When you sign an MCA agreement, you're not borrowing money in the legal sense.

You're selling a portion of your future revenue.

That one word — selling — changes how the whole product operates:

- No legal ceiling on what it can cost you
- The daily pull doesn't pause when revenue drops
- Each advance is its own deal — lenders underwrite their piece, not the total weight on your cash flow

None of this is hidden. It's in the contract.

Most business owners who took an MCA were focused on one thing: getting cash in the account fast. The structure of what they agreed to gets understood later — sometimes months later.

A $150K advance at a 1.3 factor = $195K back.

Over 6 months, that's roughly $1,477 per business day.

Stack two of those, and $2,900+ is leaving your account every single day — before payroll, rent, inventory, or anything else.

Restructuring MCA debt is different from restructuring a bank loan.

The levers are different. The negotiation looks different. Knowing that going in changes what you can actually get done.

If you're sitting on multiple MCAs right now, the path forward starts with understanding what you actually signed.
