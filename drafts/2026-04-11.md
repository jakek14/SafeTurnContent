Picture this.

A wholesale distributor. $4M a year. Steady clients. Solid margins.

Then tariffs hit — and the next decision sets off a chain he can't stop.

Product costs spike. Front-loading inventory before prices rise further looks like the smart move.

He takes an MCA for $120K. The math checks out on paper.

Daily payments: $900. Starts immediately.

Here's where it breaks:

His clients pay Net 30. Sometimes Net 45.

So for 6 weeks, $900 is leaving the account every business day while the inventory sits in a warehouse waiting to ship and get invoiced.

By week four, the operating account is bleeding. He takes a second advance to bridge it.

Now two MCAs. $1,600/day in combined withdrawals.

By month three, a third advance just to cover payroll. $2,400/day out the door — before a single employee gets paid.

The inventory bet actually worked. Revenue is up.

But the daily payments are consuming that revenue faster than cash lands.

The business isn't failing. The timing is failing.

That gap — between when money goes out and when it comes back in — is the actual problem.

Imagine restructuring all three obligations into terms that align with the actual billing cycle.

Same debt. Same clients. Same business.

Completely different math.
