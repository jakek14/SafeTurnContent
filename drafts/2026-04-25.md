The bank said no.

The MCA said yes in 48 hours.

That moment feels like a rescue. Here's what it looks like 12 months later.

Picture a manufacturer doing $4M a year — good contracts, repeat clients, 15 years in business.

Equipment fails mid-production cycle. They need $150K fast. Go to their bank — the one they've used for a decade.

Denied.

Banks are tightening right now. Economic uncertainty. Stricter underwriting. Doesn't matter how long you've been a customer.

So they take an MCA. $150K. Factor rate 1.35. Total payback: $202K. Daily pull: ~$1,150, every business day, for eight months.

Manageable. The business absorbs it.

Three months in, a large contract comes through — the kind that could be a real turning point. But the daily drain has kept cash too thin to buy the materials upfront.

Second MCA. $80K. Factor rate 1.3. Total payback: $104K. Another ~$945/day.

Now $2,100 is leaving the account every single business day.

Before payroll. Before rent. Before anything else.

That's $46,000/month in debt payments on a business doing $333K in monthly revenue.

Revenue isn't the problem. The business is doing real volume.

But the structure they built to survive the bank denial is now the thing threatening to undo it all.

This is what "just bridging the gap" looks like 12 months later.

If this scenario sounds familiar, the problem isn't your revenue. It's the structure underneath it.
