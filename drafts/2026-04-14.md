Profitable business. $3.5M in annual revenue. Payroll came up short last month.

That's not a revenue problem. That's a structure problem.

Here's how it happens:

Picture a distribution company — solid margins, repeat clients, consistent orders.

Supplier costs spike. Cash gets tight for 60 days.

The owner takes an MCA to bridge it. $75K. Daily payments: $950.

Reasonable. Short-term problem. Short-term fix.

Two months later, they apply for a bank loan to refinance out of it.

Declined. Existing debt load disqualifies them from traditional financing.

So they take a second advance to cover what the first one is draining.

Two MCAs. $1,600/day pulling from the same operating account.

That's $35,200 leaving before payroll, rent, or inventory — every single month.

Revenue is still there. Business is still running.

But the cash is gone before the owner can touch it.

Profitable on paper. Insolvent in practice.

This is how businesses doing everything right end up in crisis.

The way out isn't more capital.

It's restructuring what's already there — renegotiating with existing lenders so payments align with actual cash flow.

Same total debt. Different terms. Completely different outcome.

If the math keeps failing despite solid revenue, the structure is the problem — not the business.
