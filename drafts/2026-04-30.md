The business survived two recessions. A pandemic. Supply chain chaos.

It almost didn't survive tariffs and one client stretching payment terms by 15 days.

Imagine a wholesale distributor. $4M in revenue. 20 years in business.

They know their margins. They know their clients. They've seen hard times before.

Then tariffs hit their supply chain. Cost of goods jumps. Margins that worked for two decades get squeezed.

So they do the smart thing: buy forward inventory before the next price increase hits.

They take an MCA to fund it. Daily payment: around $1,000.

Then their biggest client — 30% of revenue — shifts payment terms from Net 30 to Net 45.

Not a lost account. Just a 15-day gap.

But that gap creates a real cash hole.

So they take a second MCA to bridge it. Daily payments climb to $1,750.

$1,750 × 22 business days = $38,500 leaving the account every month.

Before rent. Before payroll. Before anything else.

The business is still doing $4M. Still has the same clients. Still has good margins on paper.

But two external shifts — neither reckless, neither avoidable — stacked two obligations on top of cash flow that was already compressed.

The structure stopped fitting the business they were actually running.

Restructuring those obligations based on current cash flow — not what the business looked like when each advance was approved — is what changes the equation.

Same business. Same debt. Different structure. Completely different outcome.
