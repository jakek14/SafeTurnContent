MCA companies don't care if your business survives.

They care about factor rates.

Here's how it actually works:

You borrow $100K at a 1.45 factor rate.

You owe $145K total.

The lender collects that $145K through daily automated withdrawals — regardless of what your revenue looks like that month.

Had a slow week? Payments keep pulling.
Lost a major client? Payments keep pulling.
Had to shut down for a week? Payments keep pulling.

There's no interest rate. There's no APR.

There's a fixed obligation that doesn't adjust based on how your business is actually performing.

But your cash flow does.

This is how MCAs stack so fast.

When the first one starts squeezing cash, most operators do the logical thing: take a second advance to bridge the gap. The second accelerates the daily drain. Cash gets tight again. Now there's a need for a third.

Each decision made sense in isolation. The cumulative effect is brutal.

And here's what makes it worse: each lender only sees their own loan.

None of them can see the other $2,100/day being pulled from the same account. None of them are tracking the combined weight on your operating cash.

That's not malice. That's just how the industry works.

The only way out isn't more revenue.

More revenue hitting an account with $2,100 in daily pulls still doesn't give you room to operate.

The way out is restructuring the obligation stack into terms your actual cash flow can support.

Same total debt. Very different terms. Very different outcome.
