The businesses getting into trouble aren't the ones that got denied.

They're the ones that got approved — over and over again.

First MCA: made sense. Real need, real gap, fast access to capital.

Second one: covered the shortfall from the first.

Third one: covered the weight of the first two combined.

Each approval felt like a lifeline.

Each one made the next problem harder to solve.

That's the cycle. Not bad luck. Not bad decisions. Just a debt structure that looked manageable one advance at a time — until the full picture became something no business could sustain.

Here's what gets missed:

Getting approved doesn't mean you can afford it.

It means a lender ran their model and decided this deal worked for them.

Their model doesn't know about the other daily pulls already hitting your account. It doesn't know your payroll timing, your seasonal gaps, or how long your biggest clients take to pay.

It just knows you qualified.

Right now, with credit tightening and SMB bankruptcies at a 15-year high, more business owners than ever are chasing whatever approval they can find. And MCA lenders are still saying yes.

But another "yes" isn't a solution when the problem is already too many obligations.

More capital into a broken structure doesn't fix the structure.

It adds more weight to it.

The question worth asking isn't whether you can get approved.

It's what your full payment picture looks like six months after you do.
