Your accountant shows you a profitable P&L.

You stare at the screen.

And you think: then where is the money?

Revenue is up. Margins are solid. The business looks healthy on paper.

But you're manually moving money between accounts on Thursday so payroll clears on Friday.

You're pushing a vendor invoice out another 30 days.

You're watching the account drop by $1,200 every business morning before a single sale is made.

Your accountant sees profitability. You see survival.

Both of you are right.

The P&L measures what you earned. The bank account measures what you can actually use.

That's the part nobody explains.

You can run a profitable business and still feel cash-strapped — because profit is what's left after everything. Cash flow is what keeps you alive day to day.

And when daily MCA pulls, multiple lenders, and stacked obligations are all hitting the same account your business runs on — profitability doesn't protect you from the squeeze.

You don't have a revenue problem.

You have a structure problem.

The debt was likely reasonable at the time. The structure it created wasn't.

No amount of new clients will fix this — until the structure underneath gets addressed.

If you've ever stared at a profitable P&L wondering where the money went — that feeling is real. And it's fixable.
