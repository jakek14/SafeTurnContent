# Pillar 6: Hypothetical Scenario Posts

Describe realistic business situations clearly framed as hypothetical. Stories hold attention (dwell time = algorithm boost) and create emotional connection. NEVER present these as real client stories — use "Imagine...", "Picture this...", or "Here's a common scenario..." framing.

---

## Post 1: The Restaurant Owner

Picture this.

A restaurant owner doing $2.4M in annual revenue. Three locations. Growing every year.

On paper, thriving.

In reality, can't make payroll.

Here's how it happens:

Location #2 needed a buildout. Takes an MCA for $80K. Daily payments: $650.

Then equipment for location #3. Another MCA. Daily payments jump to $1,100 total.

Then a slow month hits. A third advance just to cover the gap.

Daily payments: $1,800. Every business day. Automatically pulled.

That's $39,600 leaving the account every month before rent, labor, food costs, or anything else.

A business doing $2.4M can't breathe.

Now imagine restructuring those three obligations into a single monthly payment aligned with the actual cash flow cycle.

Same total debt. Completely different experience.

That's what restructuring does.

## Post 2: The Contractor

Imagine a general contractor doing $4.5M/year.

22 employees. Fully booked for the next 6 months. Business is booming.

But here's the problem with contracting:

You pay materials and labor upfront.
Clients pay on completion. Sometimes Net 30 after that.

So the gap gets bridged with short-term financing. One loan becomes two. Two becomes four.

Now there's:
- 2 MCAs pulling daily
- 1 term loan with weekly payments
- 1 equipment loan
- 1 line of credit maxed out

Five different obligations. Five different schedules. Five different claims on the same cash.

The bookkeeper spends 2 hours every morning figuring out which accounts have enough to cover the day's withdrawals.

The business is growing. But the owner is losing sleep.

This is a scenario where consolidating the five obligations into two, renegotiating terms, and aligning payments with the project billing cycle changes everything.

The bookkeeper goes back to doing actual bookkeeping instead of financial triage.

## Post 3: The E-Commerce Brand

Here's a common scenario in e-commerce:

A brand doing $1.8M/year. Strong margins. Loyal customer base. Growing 30% year over year.

The founder takes an MCA to fund a big inventory buy for Q4.

Smart move. Q4 is their best quarter. The inventory will pay for itself 3x over.

One problem: the MCA payments start immediately.

$900/day, every business day.

But the Q4 revenue won't arrive for 2-3 months.

So for 60+ days, $900 is leaving the account daily while the inventory sits in a warehouse waiting to be sold.

By November, a second MCA just to cover operating expenses while waiting for Q4 to hit.

Now two MCAs. $1,600/day in withdrawals.

Q4 comes. Revenue is great. But the daily payments consume so much of it that January feels like there was never a good quarter.

The timing is the problem. Not the decision. Not the business.

Restructuring both MCAs into terms that match the actual sales cycle — heavier payments in Q4, lighter payments in slow months — changes the entire equation.

Same debt. Same business. Completely different outcome.

## Post 4: The Agency Owner

Picture a marketing agency. 8 people. $1.2M in revenue.

The founder is doing everything right:

Steady retainer clients. Low churn. High margins.

But she took on a line of credit to hire two senior people and a small MCA to cover a slow period.

Nothing crazy. Nothing reckless.

Then a client representing 20% of revenue churns unexpectedly.

Suddenly the payments that were "manageable" aren't.

She spends 4 months trying to replace that revenue while the payments keep pulling.

Now she's 3 months from having to let someone go — not because business is bad, but because the payment structure assumed revenue that's no longer there.

Renegotiating terms with both lenders based on current revenue — not peak revenue — could reduce payments by 40% per month. Same total obligation, extended timeline.

She keeps her team. Replaces the client. Gets back to growing.

The business doesn't need saving. The structure just needs to match reality.

## Post 5: The Family Business

Imagine a second-generation manufacturing business.

Dad built it from nothing. Son took over 3 years ago.

Revenue: $6M. 30 employees. 40-year client relationships.

But Dad had taken on debt to fund an expansion right before handing over the reins. Two term loans and an equipment lease.

The son inherits a business that looks great — but has $18K/month in debt payments baked in.

When raw material costs spike, that $18K becomes unbearable.

He doesn't want to tell his father. Doesn't want to be the one who "couldn't handle it."

So he takes an MCA to bridge the gap.

Then another.

Now the business his father built is bleeding $42K/month in combined debt payments.

This is where restructuring the entire obligation stack — working with every creditor, the original lenders and the MCA companies — can reduce monthly payments by more than half.

The business stabilizes. The structure matches reality again.

Debt isn't a character flaw. It's a structural problem that compounds when you try to solve it alone.
