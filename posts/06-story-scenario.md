## Post 1: The Restaurant Owner

A restaurant owner reached out to us last month.

$2.4M in annual revenue. Three locations. Growing every year.

On paper, thriving.

In reality, he couldn't make payroll.

Here's what happened:

Location #2 needed a buildout. He took an MCA for $80K. Daily payments: $650.

Then he needed equipment for location #3. Another MCA. Daily payments jumped to $1,100 total.

Then a slow month hit. He took a third advance just to cover the gap.

Daily payments: $1,800. Every business day. Automatically pulled.

That's $39,600 leaving his account every month before rent, labor, food costs, or anything else.

A business doing $2.4M couldn't breathe.

We restructured the three obligations into a single monthly payment aligned with his actual cash flow cycle.

Same total debt. Completely different experience.

He called us 60 days later: "For the first time in a year, I know I can make payroll next Friday."

That's what restructuring does.

## Post 2: The Contractor

A general contractor came to us doing $4.5M/year.

22 employees. Fully booked for the next 6 months. Business was booming.

But here's the problem with contracting:

You pay materials and labor upfront.
Clients pay on completion. Sometimes Net 30 after that.

So he'd been bridging the gap with short-term financing. One loan became two. Two became four.

By the time he called us, he had:
- 2 MCAs pulling daily
- 1 term loan with weekly payments
- 1 equipment loan
- 1 line of credit maxed out

Five different obligations. Five different schedules. Five different claims on his cash.

His bookkeeper was spending 2 hours every morning figuring out which accounts had enough to cover the day's withdrawals.

The business was growing. But the owner was losing sleep.

We consolidated the five obligations into two, renegotiated terms, and aligned payments with his project billing cycle.

Within 90 days, his bookkeeper went back to doing actual bookkeeping instead of financial triage.

## Post 3: The E-Commerce Brand

An e-commerce brand doing $1.8M/year.

Strong margins. Loyal customer base. Growing 30% year over year.

The founder took an MCA to fund a big inventory buy for Q4.

Smart move. Q4 was their best quarter. The inventory would pay for itself 3x over.

One problem: the MCA payments started immediately.

$900/day, every business day.

But the Q4 revenue wouldn't arrive for 2-3 months.

So for 60+ days, $900 was leaving the account daily while the inventory sat in a warehouse waiting to be sold.

By November, they took a second MCA just to cover operating expenses while waiting for Q4 to hit.

Now they had two MCAs. $1,600/day in withdrawals.

Q4 came. Revenue was great. But the daily payments consumed so much of it that January felt like they'd never had a good quarter.

The timing was the problem. Not the decision. Not the business.

We restructured both MCAs into terms that matched their actual sales cycle — heavier payments in Q4, lighter payments in slow months.

Same debt. Same business. Completely different outcome.

## Post 4: The Agency Owner

A marketing agency. 8 people. $1.2M in revenue.

The founder was doing everything right:

Steady retainer clients. Low churn. High margins.

But she'd taken on a line of credit to hire two senior people and a small MCA to cover a slow period.

Nothing crazy. Nothing reckless.

Then a client representing 20% of revenue churned unexpectedly.

Suddenly the payments that were "manageable" weren't.

She spent the next 4 months trying to replace that revenue while the payments kept pulling.

By the time she called us, she was 3 months from having to let someone go — not because business was bad, but because the payment structure assumed revenue that was no longer there.

We renegotiated terms with both lenders based on her current revenue, not her peak revenue.

New payment structure: 40% less per month. Same total obligation, extended timeline.

She kept her team. Replaced the client. And got back to growing.

The business didn't need to be saved. The structure just needed to match reality.

## Post 5: The Family Business

A second-generation manufacturing business.

Dad built it from nothing. Son took over 3 years ago.

Revenue: $6M. 30 employees. 40-year client relationships.

But Dad had taken on debt to fund an expansion right before handing over the reins. Two term loans and an equipment lease.

Son inherited a business that looked great — but had $18K/month in debt payments baked in.

When raw material costs spiked, that $18K became unbearable.

He didn't want to tell his father. He didn't want to be the one who "couldn't handle it."

So he took an MCA to bridge the gap.

Then another.

By the time he called us, the business his father built was bleeding $42K/month in combined debt payments.

We worked with every creditor — the original lenders and the MCA companies.

Restructured the entire obligation stack. Reduced monthly payments by 55%.

The business stabilized. The son told his father what happened.

His dad said: "I would have done the same thing."

Debt isn't a character flaw. It's a structural problem that compounds when you try to solve it alone.
