Challenge conventional wisdom about business debt, cash flow, and what "help" actually looks like.

These posts should make people stop and think "wait... is that true?" They create engagement through disagreement and reframes.

## Post 1: Revenue Isn't the Answer

You don't need more revenue.

I know that sounds wrong.

Every advisor, every mentor, every business book says "grow your way out of it."

But here's what actually happens when a business with broken debt structure tries to grow:

Revenue goes up.
Payments go up faster.
Cash gets tighter.

Because every new dollar that comes in is already claimed by 3 different lenders before it hits your operating account.

More revenue into a broken structure doesn't fix the structure.

It just makes the problem bigger.

The businesses that actually stabilize?

They don't start with "how do I make more money?"

They start with "where is my money actually going — and how do I change that?"

That's the question that changes everything.

## Post 2: Your Accountant Can't See This

Your accountant looks at your P&L and says "you're profitable."

You look at your bank account and wonder how that's possible.

Here's why:

Profit is an accounting concept.
Cash flow is a survival concept.

Your P&L doesn't show:
- Daily MCA withdrawals hitting your account
- Stacked payment schedules draining cash before you can use it
- The gap between when you earn and when you actually receive

Profitability on paper means nothing if cash isn't available when you need it.

Most accountants aren't trained to look at debt structure. They see revenue, expenses, and profit.

They don't see the timing problem that's slowly choking your business.

If your books say you're doing fine but your bank account disagrees — it's not bad management.

It's a structural problem. And it needs a structural solution.

## Post 3: Loans Don't Fix Debt Problems

Taking on another loan to cover existing debt is like using a credit card to pay a credit card.

It feels like relief.

For about 2 weeks.

Then you have the original problem plus a new payment.

I see this pattern constantly:

Business gets behind on payments.
They apply for a new loan or MCA to cover the gap.
New lender says yes.
Now they have 4 lenders instead of 3.
Cash gets tighter.
Repeat.

Each round makes the next one worse.

The real solution isn't more capital — it's fewer obligations pulling from the same cash flow.

Consolidation. Renegotiation. Restructuring.

Not another layer of debt on top of debt.

If you're thinking about applying for a new loan just to make payments on an old one — pause.

There's a better path.

## Post 4: Hard Work Won't Save You Here

"Just work harder."

That advice works for a lot of business problems.

This isn't one of them.

When your debt structure is broken, working harder means:

More sales → more cash in → more cash immediately pulled out by lenders → same stress

You can't outwork a structural problem.

A business owner doing $3M/year called us last quarter. Working 80-hour weeks. Team of 15. Growing every month.

Couldn't make payroll every other Friday.

Not because the business was failing. Because 5 different payment obligations were pulling from the same account on different days, and the timing never lined up.

The fix wasn't more hustle.

It was restructuring when and how those payments hit.

Within 60 days, same revenue, same expenses — but cash was actually available when needed.

That's the difference between a revenue problem and a structure problem.

## Post 5: The "Good Debt" Myth

There's a myth in business that some debt is "good debt."

Growth capital. Equipment financing. Lines of credit.

And sure — when structured correctly, debt can be a tool.

But here's what nobody talks about:

Good debt becomes bad debt the moment payment timing doesn't match cash flow.

A perfectly reasonable loan at 8% becomes a business killer when the monthly payment hits 3 days before your biggest receivable clears.

It's not the interest rate that destroys businesses.

It's the timing.

Two businesses can have the exact same debt load. One thrives, one suffocates.

The difference? How payments are structured relative to when cash actually comes in.

If your "good debt" is making it hard to operate — it's not good debt anymore.

It's a structural mismatch that needs to be fixed.
