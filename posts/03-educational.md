## Post 1: 3 Signs Your Debt Structure Is Killing Your Business

3 signs your debt structure is slowly killing your business:

1. You're profitable on paper but can't cover next week's expenses.

Your P&L says one thing. Your bank account says another. That gap? It's almost always a payment timing issue — not a revenue issue.

2. You have 3+ lenders pulling from the same account.

Each one made sense individually. But together, they create a daily cash drain that leaves nothing for actual operations. Stacked payments are the #1 structural killer for growing businesses.

3. You've taken new debt to cover old debt more than once.

This is the cycle that accelerates the problem. Each new layer adds another payment, another withdrawal, another claim on your cash before you can use it.

If you checked 2 or all 3 of these — your problem isn't the business.

It's the structure underneath it.

And structures can be rebuilt.

## Post 2: MCA 101 — What They Don't Tell You

Merchant Cash Advances aren't loans.

Most business owners don't realize this until it's too late.

Here's what makes MCAs different — and dangerous:

They buy your future revenue. You're not borrowing money. You're selling tomorrow's income at a discount today.

They pull daily. Not monthly like a loan. Every single business day, money leaves your account. That's 20-22 withdrawals per month.

Factor rates ≠ interest rates. A 1.3 factor rate sounds low. It's actually 50-80% APR when you do the math on a 6-month term.

They stack. One MCA leads to another. Lenders know this. Some count on it. Before you know it, 3-4 MCAs are pulling simultaneously.

There's no regulation. MCAs aren't classified as loans, so consumer lending protections don't apply.

None of this means an MCA is always wrong. But most business owners don't understand what they agreed to until the daily withdrawals start hurting.

If you're in an MCA and the payments are choking your business — there are options.

## Post 3: The Cash Flow Timing Problem

Most cash flow problems aren't about how much money you make.

They're about when the money moves.

Here's a simple example:

You invoice a client on March 1.
They pay Net 30 — so cash arrives April 1.

But your MCA payment hits March 5.
Payroll hits March 15.
Rent hits March 1.
Vendor payments hit March 10 and March 20.

All your expenses are in March.
Your revenue arrives in April.

Same money. Same amounts. But the timing creates a gap that feels like you're going broke.

Now multiply this across 10 clients, 4 lenders, and 15 employees.

That's how a $3M business can't make payroll.

The fix isn't more revenue. It's realigning when money goes out with when money comes in.

This is what debt restructuring actually does — it doesn't make debt disappear. It makes the timing work.

## Post 4: What Creditor Negotiation Actually Looks Like

Most people think creditor negotiation means begging for mercy.

It's not.

Here's what it actually looks like when done right:

Step 1: Full picture.
Map every obligation — amounts, terms, payment schedules, penalties. Most business owners don't have this in one place. That's the first problem.

Step 2: Prioritization.
Not all creditors are equal. Some have legal leverage. Some don't. Some will negotiate. Some won't unless approached correctly.

Step 3: Structured proposal.
You don't call and say "I can't pay." You present a restructured payment plan that shows how they get paid — just on terms the business can actually sustain.

Step 4: Execution.
New terms get documented. Payments get realigned. Cash flow opens up.

The key insight: creditors would rather get paid on adjusted terms than not get paid at all. Most of them know this. They just need a structured conversation, not a panicked phone call.

## Post 5: The Stacking Problem Explained

Here's how most businesses end up with 4+ lenders without planning to:

Month 1: Business needs $50K for inventory. Gets an MCA. Daily payments of $400. Manageable.

Month 4: Cash gets tight. Another lender offers $30K. Daily payments now $650 total. Still survivable.

Month 7: Big client pays late. Need to cover payroll. Third MCA. Daily payments now $1,100.

Month 10: Can't cover the triple daily pull. Fourth lender offers to "consolidate" — but really just adds another layer. Daily payments: $1,400.

In 10 months, this business went from one manageable payment to $1,400 leaving their account every single business day.

That's $30,800/month before rent, payroll, or operations.

This is called stacking. And it happens to smart, well-run businesses more often than anyone admits.

The exit isn't another loan. It's restructuring what already exists.
